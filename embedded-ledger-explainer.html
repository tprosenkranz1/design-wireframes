Designing Now

The Two Things We're Designing Right Away

Out of the Apr 15 meeting in Belvedere Castle with Anwar, Mike, and Kirill: the journal entry page and the review/approve flows for billing (AR) and costs (AP) come before anything else. Here's what each looks like and how they fit with what Cru already does.

The Mental Model

Cru already ingests data from three pipes. The design work now is making the human-in-the-loop approval real, then funneling everything into the right journal entries so the ledger stays accurate.

AR / INCOMING
AIAs picked up via email connector + Textura integration
AP / OUTGOING
Vendor bills picked up via email + matched against POs
CASH / MOVEMENTS
Bank transactions via Plaid, categorized by AI agents
↓
Human Review & Approval (new)
Edit / approve / reject / pick COA account / pick project dimension
↓
Journal Entry - posted to Sage GL
DR / CR balanced, with project + contract + division dimensions
The key insight from Wednesday's meeting: journal entries aren't a back-office afterthought. They're the connective tissue between every Cru data source (billing, bills, cash) and the ledger. Every other surface we build either produces journal entries or reads from them.

1 · Journal Entry Page

This is the surface where any journal entry gets created, reviewed, or adjusted. Most entries will be auto-generated (from billing approval, bill approval, or a cash transaction), but the page also supports manual entries for things like month-end accruals, reclassifications, and corrections.

What Mike told us has to be here: ability to pull from the chart of accounts on both the debit and credit side, and the ability to associate the entry with project characteristics (contract, division, geography). Without these, the entry is useless for a construction sub.

What each field does

Header (top row)

  • Date - which period this hits
  • Reference - auto-generated ID (JE-YYYY-####)
  • Source - where this entry originated (Transactions tab, Billing approval, Bill approval, or "Manual")
  • Memo - human-readable note that follows the entry through reports

Line items (table)

  • Account - typeahead dropdown pulling the full COA tree
  • Description - per-line context (shows up on drill-through)
  • Debit / Credit - amount on one side or the other, never both
  • Project / Division - Sage dimensions. Required if the account is a job cost or revenue line

Core functionality (not negotiable)

  • Auto-balance check. The Approve button is disabled until debits equal credits. The totals row shows the running sum live.
  • COA typeahead. Typing "mat" surfaces "5200 - Materials." Typing an account number jumps to it directly.
  • Dimension inheritance. Set the project once on the header and every line inherits it (can be overridden per line).
  • Multi-line support. An entry can have 2 lines or 20. The AR invoice with retainage needs 3 lines. A month-end close entry might need 15.
  • Reversal. Any posted entry can be reversed, which creates a mirror entry with DRs and CRs flipped. Original entry stays visible in the audit trail.
  • Attachments. Support attaching the source document (AIA PDF, vendor bill image, bank statement) to the entry.
  • Approval gate. Only approved entries get posted to Sage. Drafts live in Cru only.
Open design question: Do we show the journal entry page inside the existing Transactions tab as a side-panel, or is it its own top-level Journal Entries tab? Leaning toward: Journal Entries tab in the sidebar, with the Transactions tab linking into it via the "suggest journal entry" action (see section 4 below).

4 · How Transactions Tab and Journal Entries Fit Together

Tommy's open question: there's overlap between the Transactions tab (which already exists) and the new Journal Entry section. Here's the proposed split.

Transactions Tab (today)

The cash-level view. Every bank transaction that comes through Plaid shows up here. AI agents categorize them. This is where bookkeeping-adjacent work happens day to day.

  • Bank ins/outs
  • Credit card transactions
  • AI-suggested category
  • Per-transaction edit

Journal Entries (new)

The ledger-level view. Every accounting event, cash or not. Automated entries (from Billing / AP / Transactions) plus manual entries for accruals, reclassifications, and adjustments.

  • AR journals from billing approvals
  • AP journals from bill approvals
  • Cash journals from categorized transactions
  • Manual entries for month-end

The bridge: Transactions recommends Journal Entries

Rather than having two separate surfaces people have to switch between, the Transactions tab becomes the trigger and the Journal Entry page becomes the workspace. When the AI agent categorizes a transaction, it drafts the journal entry behind the scenes. If the entry is straightforward (DR expense / CR bank with high confidence), it auto-posts. If it's ambiguous, it shows up as a suggestion in the Journal Entry queue.

Decision this unblocks: the Transactions tab stays roughly what it is today, with one additional surface: a "Suggest Journal Entry" action per row (and a small banner on the row if the AI isn't confident). The Journal Entries page becomes the home for anything that needs a human decision. This way we avoid building two redundant editors for the same data.

5 · Where Reporting Lives (Future, But Worth Anchoring)

Everything above (journal entries, AR approval, AP approval) exists to keep the ledger accurate. The ledger exists to power reports. Reporting isn't in the immediate design focus, but the shape of what's coming affects what we build now.

WIP Report

The #1 construction report. Needs accurate revenue + earned cost per project. Drives straight off approved AR journals (revenue) and approved AP journals (cost), filtered by project dimension.

Balance Sheet

Assets / Liabilities / Equity snapshot. Sage auto-generates this from the ledger. Requires nothing new from us once journal entries post correctly.

Income Statement (P&L)

Revenue - expenses over a period. Already available in Tier 1 demo. Gets more accurate as real journals flow in.

AR / AP Aging

Bucket unpaid invoices / bills by age. Derived from AR / AP journals and the invoice / bill state machines.

Why this matters right now: if we design journal entries without dimensions baked in, reporting is broken. That's why Mike was emphatic about project / contract / division / geography showing up on every line. Every shortcut we take on journal entry metadata becomes a broken report later.

Chart of Accounts (COA) View CRU-628

The chart of accounts is the filing system for all financial transactions. Every dollar gets categorized into one of these buckets. There are five top-level categories that every business in the world uses:

What you OWN and what you're OWED

  • Assets - Cash in bank, money customers owe you (AR), equipment, retainage held back

What you OWE

  • Liabilities - Bills you haven't paid yet (AP), loans, credit cards
  • Equity - Owner's investment and retained profits

Money coming IN

  • Income/Revenue - Contract revenue, change order revenue

Money going OUT

  • Expenses - Labor, materials, subcontractor costs, equipment rental, overhead

For construction, we need sub-accounts under these. Here's a simplified example of what the COA tree looks like:

1000 - Assets
1100 - Accounts Receivable (money GCs owe you)
1110 - Current AR (due within normal terms)
1120 - Retainage Receivable (held back until project closes)
1200 - Cash & Bank
1210 - Operating Account
1220 - Payroll Account
2000 - Liabilities
2100 - Accounts Payable (what you owe suppliers)
2200 - Accrued Payroll
3000 - Equity
3100 - Owner's Equity
3200 - Retained Earnings
4000 - Revenue
4100 - Contract Revenue
4200 - Change Order Revenue
5000 - Expenses
5100 - Labor
5200 - Materials
5300 - Subcontractor Costs
5400 - Equipment
5500 - Overhead
The COA is like the folder structure on your computer. You don't dump every file on your desktop - you organize them into folders. The COA tells the ledger which "folder" each dollar belongs in.

OAuth Token Management CRU-598

Before Cru can talk to Sage's servers, it needs to prove it's allowed to. This is called authentication. Sage uses a system called OAuth 2.0 with machine-to-machine credentials.

Think of it like a keycard for a building. Cru has a client ID (the card) and a client secret (the PIN). Every 8 hours, the card expires and Cru needs to silently get a new one. The user never sees this happening.

LedgerService - The Middle Layer CRU-607 / CRU-608 / CRU-609

This is the most important architectural piece. The LedgerService sits between Cru's product code and Sage's APIs. It translates between "Cru language" and "Sage language."

Why not just call Sage directly? Two reasons:

Abstraction

If we ever swap Sage for a different ledger provider, we only change the LedgerService - not every piece of code that touches accounting.

Domain language

Cru's code says "postARInvoice()" - plain English. The LedgerService translates that into Sage's specific API format with the right account codes, dimensions, and entity references.

Write interface (CRU-607) - methods that push data INTO Sage:

  • postARInvoice() - record that a customer owes you money
  • postAPBill() - record that you owe a supplier money
  • postExpense() - record a cost
  • postBankTransaction() - record money moving in/out of the bank
  • reverseEntry() - undo a mistake

Read interface (CRU-608) - methods that pull data FROM Sage:

  • getARBalance() - how much are customers owing you?
  • getAPBalance() - how much do you owe suppliers?
  • getRetainageBalance() - how much retainage is being held?
  • getJobCosts() - how much have you spent on a project?
  • getCashPosition() - how much cash do you have right now?

Double-Entry Accounting & Journal Entries CRU-612 / CRU-613

This is the most important accounting concept to understand. Every financial event gets recorded as a journal entry with at least two lines that must balance. One line is a debit (DR), the other is a credit (CR). The total debits must always equal the total credits.

Think of it like a see-saw. Every time money moves, you add weight to both sides equally. The see-saw always stays balanced. If you bill a customer $10,000, one side records "they owe me $10,000" (debit AR) and the other records "I earned $10,000" (credit Revenue). Same dollar, two perspectives.

Example: You submit a $100,000 invoice to a GC, with 10% retainage

The GC will pay you $90,000 now and hold back $10,000 until the project is done. This single event creates a 3-line journal entry:

AccountDebit (DR)Credit (CR)Why
1100 Accounts Receivable $90,000 GC owes you this now
1120 Retainage Receivable $10,000 GC holds this back
4100 Contract Revenue $100,000 You earned this
Total $100,000 $100,000 Balanced

CRU-612 automates this. When a sub submits an invoice through Cru's Billing tab, the LedgerService automatically posts this journal to Sage. The sub doesn't touch anything.

Example: The GC pays that $90,000 invoice

AccountDebit (DR)Credit (CR)Why
1200 Operating Bank $90,000 Cash arrived in your bank
1100 Accounts Receivable $90,000 GC no longer owes you
Total $90,000 $90,000 Balanced

CRU-613 automates this. When a payment comes in (detected via Plaid or manual entry), the journal posts automatically.

AI Agents Reading from Sage CRU-624 / CRU-625 / CRU-627

Today, Cru's AI agents work off data in Cru's own database. In Tier 3, they switch to reading from the Sage ledger - the authoritative financial record. This makes them dramatically more accurate.

Job Costing Agent (CRU-624)

Reads actual costs and revenue per project from Sage using dimension filters. Compares against estimates to flag projects going over budget. Today it estimates from bank transactions - with Sage it has real posted costs.

Collections Agent (CRU-625)

Reads AR balance per GC from Sage. Knows exactly what each GC owes including retainage. Today it sums unpaid invoices - with Sage it has the authoritative ledger balance including partial payments and adjustments.

Cash Forecasting Agent (CRU-627)

Reads current cash, open AR, open AP, and retainage from Sage. Predicts when money will come in and go out. With real ledger data, forecasts move from "good guesses" to "financial projections."

Why This Matters

An agent that says "you have $50k in AR" based on unpaid invoices might be wrong if there were partial payments or adjustments. An agent reading from the ledger is reading the single source of truth.

WIP Report (Work in Progress) Working Doc

The WIP report is the holy grail of construction accounting. It's the report that answers: on each project, are you ahead or behind financially?

Here's the core idea. On any project, there are two numbers that matter:

Billings

How much you've invoiced the GC so far

Earned Revenue

How much you've actually "earned" based on % of work completed

If you've billed more than you've earned, you're overbilled (you got paid ahead of the work). If you've billed less than you've earned, you're underbilled (you did work you haven't been paid for yet). Neither is inherently bad, but you need to know which one you are.

Project Contract % Complete Earned Revenue Billed to Date Over/(Under)
Oak Street Office $500,000 60% $300,000 $275,000 ($25,000)
Pine Ave School $800,000 40% $320,000 $360,000 $40,000
Elm St Datacenter $1,200,000 25% $300,000 $300,000 $0

Oak Street: underbilled by $25k (did more work than you've billed for)
Pine Ave: overbilled by $40k (billed ahead of the work)

Most of our target subs can't produce this report. When a GC asks for a WIP, they hand over their outstanding AR. If Cru can generate a real WIP automatically from the ledger data, that alone could be the killer feature.

Sage doesn't have this report natively in their embedded services. We'll either build it ourselves from ledger data (preferred approach per the working doc) or work with Sage to add it.

Balance Sheet & Cash Flow Statement Working Doc

These complete the trio of standard financial statements (along with the P&L).

Balance Sheet

A snapshot of the business at a single point in time. It answers: what do you own, what do you owe, and what's left over?

Assets = Liabilities + Equity

This equation MUST always balance (that's why it's called a balance sheet).

Cash Flow Statement

Shows where cash actually came from and where it went over a period. A business can be profitable on the P&L but still run out of cash (common in construction because of long payment cycles and retainage).

Three sections: Operating, Investing, Financing.
